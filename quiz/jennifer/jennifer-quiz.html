Case Study: Jennifer

Jennifer is a 27 year old woman, gravida 10000 at 39 0/7 weeks gestation. She has no surgical history, and her medical history is unremarkable. Jennifer is a CNA who works the night shift at a skilled nursing facility.

Jennifer presented to OB triage at 1400 complaining of low back pain and decreased fetal movement for the last 24 hours. She worked night shift the night before coming to triage, and she had not eaten for more than 16 hours. Her vital signs were as follows: temperature oral, 98.6° F (37° C); blood pressure, 116/64 mmHg; pulse, 78 bpm; and, respirations, 20 breaths per minute. Jennifer preferred to lie on her right side. The U/S and tocodynamometer were applied.

The fetal baseline was 145 bpm; variability, minimal; no accelerations; and a deceleration to 130 bpm that was gradual in onset but the type of deceleration (early or late) could not be determined because uterine activity was not recording clearly.

Jennifer reports smoking 1 pack of cigarettes per day, and denies using any OTC medications, illicit substances, or alcohol. Jennifer’s pre-pregnancy BMI was 18. She gained 8 pounds during her pregnancy; less than the Institute of Medicine's recommended weight gain of 28–40 pounds for an underweight woman with a singleton pregnancy. The first trimester ultrasound was consistent with Jennifer's last menstrual period. Second trimester screenings were normal and the ultrasound revealed normal anatomy. Fetal growth restriction was diagnosed during an ultrasound at 38 weeks and twice weekly NST were ordered. Jennifer's induction was scheduled for 39 2/7 weeks.

The vaginal examination (VE) revealed a cervix that was 2cm dilated, 70% effaced, and a fetal vertex at 0 station. The nurse performed fetal scalp stimulation during the VE in an attempt to stimulate an acceleration. There was no FHR response to scalp stimulation. The RN again adjusted the tocodynamometer.

The RN notified the provider of the VE, FHR pattern, absent FHR response to scalp stimulation and requested a bedside evaluation. The provider requested that Jennifer be admitted to L&D and IV access obtained, and stated that she would arrive to evaluate Jennifer in 15 minutes. The triage nurse initiated Jennifer's IV access.

The triage nurse transferred Jennifer to L&D at 1435, and provided a hand-off report to the L&D nurse. The L&D nurse assisted Jennifer to a lateral position, and applied the U/S and tocodynamometer.

Only respond to these questions in relation to the segments of Jennifer's tracing between 1441 and 1451:

1) What is the baseline fetal heart rate?

Answer: 145 - Approximate mean FHR rounded to increments of 5 bpm during a 10-minute window excluding accelerations and decelerations and periods of marked variability. There must be ≥ 2 minutes of identifiable baseline segments.

2) What is the variability?

Answer: Minimal - Amplitude range visually detectable but ≤ 5 beats per minute.

3) Are accelerations present?

Answer: No.

4) What is the frequency of the contractions?

Answer: 2-4 minutes - Contraction frequency is measured from the beginning of one contraction to the beginning of the next and is expressed in minutes.

5) What is the duration of the contractions?

Answer: 70-80 seconds - Duration is the length of the contraction and is described in seconds.

6) What is the category?

Answer: Category II, indeterminate - Variability is minimal and decelerations are present.

7) What type of periodic change occurred?

Answer: Late decelerations.

8) Which physiologic goals need to be addressed?

Answers:
Maximize uteroplacental blood flow.
Maximize oxygenation.
Maximize umbilical circulation.

What clinical interventions and communications are appropriate in the presence of these tracing characteristics and the assessment information about Jennifer?

9) Is internal fetal monitoring needed (FSE or IUPC)?

Answer: No - The external monitor is recording a fetal tracing that can be evaluated. An interpretable fetal tracing is important for the assessment of fetal status so that the need for uterine resuscitation can be determined. The least invasive mode of monitoring should be used. External monitoring should be adjusted to obtain an interpretable tracing; internal monitoring is generally not needed. Administration of an aminoinfusion requires an IUPC.

10) Change the mother's position?

Answer: Yes - When the mother is in a lateral position (right or left) or another non-supine position the uterus does not compress the vena cava or aorta; thus, maternal cardiac return and cardiac output are maximized, and blood flow to the uterus is optimal. Changing positions may also alleviate cord compression, if present.

11) Fluid bolus of at least 500 mL of lactated Ringers solution if not contraindicated?

Answer: Yes - Jennifer has no contraindications; her intake and output should also be taken into consideration. There are data to suggest that increasing IV fluids will positively affect uterine blood flow and thus fetal oxygenation, even in women who are normotensive and well hydrated. A fluid bolus may decrease uterine activity and increases maternal blood volume.

12) Administer supplemental oxygen at 10 L/min using non-rebreather mask and discontinue as soon as possible?

Answer: Yes - Because the fetal tracing characteristics include minimal variability with late decelerations, it is appropriate to administer oxygen at 10L/minute via non-rebreather face mask rather than waiting for response to the less invasive interventions. It is important to discontinue oxygen as soon as possible based on the fetal response.

13) Decrease or discontinue oxytocin?

Answer: No - Oxytocin is not being administered at this time.

14) Administer tocolytic (e.g., terbutaline)?

Answer: No - Tocolytics may be administered as a temporary intrauterine resuscitation measure to reduce uterine activity.

15) Perform fetal scalp stimulation?

Answer: No - Fetal scalp stimulation was performed in OB triage with no fetal heart rate acceleration. Fetal scalp stimulation is an indirect method used to evaluate oxygenation and acid-base status. An acceleration is associated with fetal-well-being; however, absence of acceleration does not predict fetal compromise. Fetal scalp stimulation is performed during segments of baseline, not during decelerations.

16) Correct maternal hypotension?

Answer: No - Hypotension is not present at this time. Hypotension decreases uteroplacental perfusion which becomes readily apparent in the fetal heart rate.

17) Administer a vasopressor (e.g., ephedrine)?

Answer: No - Ephedrine may be administered when a mother has hypotension secondary to regional analgesia or anesthesia.

18) Administer an amnioinfusion?

Answer: No - Amnioinfusion is not indicated in Jennifer's case at this time as there are no variable decelerations. Amnioinfusion may be administered in an attempt to resolve variable decelerations. These decelerations are often caused by umbilical cord compression due to decreased amniotic fluid.

19) Notify primary care provider about findings and tracing?

Answer: Yes - The physician should be notified about the EFM tracing characteristics. In some situations, it is optimal for the clinician to remain at the bedside and request that someone else notify the primary care provider to request a bedside evaluation. The immediacy of the request is determined by the clinical situation. Some situations may require changes in the plan of care while others require bedside evaluation.

20) Notify the charge nurse?

Answer: Yes - Advise the charge nurse about Jennifer's fetal tracing and whether uterine resuscitative measures improved the fetal tracing. The charge nurse can coordinate or assist with communication with the perinatal team (pediatric/neonatal team, surgical team), if needed. She may also assist with or direct another staff member to assist with interventions, as needed.

21) Support maternal coping?

Answer: Yes - Calmly explain interventions as they are provided in an effort to enhance her understanding of the situation and to decrease potential anxiety.

The physician arrived at 1450 and at 1500 decided to proceed with a cesarean birth because Jennifer was remote from vaginal delivery and the final EFM tracing was trending from a Category II to Category III. Jennifer underwent a primary cesarean birth under spinal anesthesia. There was thick meconium at birth.

The infant was non-vigorous and endotracheal suctioning obtained meconium fluid below the cords. The infant required positive pressure ventilation vie T-piece resuscitator with room air and then an increase to 40% oxygen at 2 minutes. Positive end expiratory pressure was maintained after spontaneous ventilations were established. The infant was admitted to the NICU. Arterial cord gas values revealed: pH = 7.00, pO2 = 18 mmHg, pCO2 = 72 mmHg; Bicarbonate = 19 mEq/L; Base Deficit = 16 mEq/L. This revealed mixed acidosis.
