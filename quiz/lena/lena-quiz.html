Case Study: Lena

Lena, a 34 year old woman, gravida 1 at 39 0/7 weeks gestation, presented to the labor and delivery unit at 1015. Her water broke at 0600 am this morning with clear fluid. Lena stated that her contractions were irregular, about 7 to 15 minutes apart. Her medical history was unremarkable, and she had no surgical history. The first trimester ultrasound revealed a pregnancy consistent with dates; second trimester screenings, normal; Rho(D) immune globin administered at 28 weeks. Her pre-pregnancy BMI was 20.2, and she gained 22 pounds during this pregnancy, within the recommended weight gain for a woman of normal weight.

The nurse's initial assessment findings were: Blood pressure 132/74 mmHg, temperature 98.6° F (37° C); pulse 72 bpm; respirations 20; FHR, 150 bpm; moderate variability; acceleration noted; one contraction in the first 20 minutes, duration 40 seconds, palpated as mild. VE revealed a cervix 2 cm dilated, 75% effaced, and fetal head at -2 station. The nurse called Lena's primary OB provider to report Lena's history and findings. The health care provider requested Lena be admitted for induction for PROM at term. The unit was busy, the induction schedule was full with medically indicated inductions, and the nurse knew at least two other women admitted within the last 2 hours also needed medically indicated inductions. The nurse offered to transfer the provider to the charge nurse to discuss options for how to accommodate Lena. The charge nurse told the health care provider that Lena's induction would probably be started by 3 pm because additional nurses were coming in to staff, and 3 patients would likely deliver over the next 2 hours.

The nurse removed the external monitor to allow Lena to walk. At 1440 the charge nurse determined that staffing was adequate and Lena's induction could proceed. The nurse started an IV and asked Lena about her contractions. Lena stated that her contractions were the same as when she arrived. The nurse applied the external monitor to record FHR and contractions. The fetal heart baseline was 150 bpm; variability, moderate; no accelerations; no decelerations. No contractions were recorded before the induction began and Lena's uterus was soft when palpated and her fetus active.

Lena's induction started at 1515, and her vital signs were: blood pressure 128/70 mmHg, temperature 98.6° F (37° C); pulse 74 bpm; respirations 18. By 1710 Lena's contractions were every 2- 3 minutes, duration 50-70 seconds; and palpated as moderate. Lena was tolerating the contractions well. Fetal heart rate baseline was 145 bpm and variability moderate and no accelerations. Several variable decelerations to 100 bpm had occurred, and position changes had alleviated them. A VE revealed the cervix had effaced to 100%; the cervix remained 2 cm dilated, and fetal head at -2 station. Lena's vital signs were: blood pressure 134/74 mmHg, temperature 98.6° F (37° C); pulse 78 bpm; respirations 22.

At 1750 a variable deceleration to 90 bpm from a baseline of 145 bpm occurred. Despite changing Lena's position, variable decelerations to 85-90 bpm continued with the next 2 contractions. The nurse changed Lena's position again, and administered an IV fluid bolus of 500 mL. She called the primary health care provider who reviewed the tracing from his office; the charge nurse came to the room. The provider indicated he would arrive at the hospital in about 20 minutes to insert an IUPC for an amnioinfusion.

Only respond to these questions in relation to the segments of Lena's tracing between 1755-1805:

1) What is the baseline fetal heart rate?

Answer: 145 - Approximate mean FHR rounded to increments of 5 bpm during a 10-minute window excluding accelerations and decelerations and periods of marked variability. There must be ≥ 2 minutes of identifiable baseline segments.

2) What is the variability?

Answer: Minimal - Amplitude range visually detectable but ≤ 5 beats per minute.

3) Are accelerations present?

Answer: No.

4) What is the frequency of the contractions?

Answer: 2-3 minutes - Contraction frequency is measured from the beginning of one contraction to the beginning of the next and is expressed in minutes.

5) What is the duration of the contractions?

Answer: 50-80 seconds - Duration is the length of the contraction and is described in seconds.

6) What is the category?

Answer: Category II, indeterminate - The variability is minimal and decelerations are present.

7) What type of periodic change occurred?

Answer: Variable decelerations.

8) Which physiologic goals need to be addressed?

Please select all that apply.

Answers:
Maximize uteroplacental blood flow.
Maximize oxygenation.
Maximize umbilical circulation.
Support maternal coping.

What clinical interventions and communications are appropriate in the presence of these tracing characteristics and the assessment information about Lena?

9) Is internal fetal monitoring needed (FSE or IUPC)?

Answer: Yes - An IUPC is needed for an amnioinfusion. External monitoring is recording so this is an interpretable tracing. An interpretable fetal tracing is important for the assessment of fetal status so that the need for uterine resuscitation can be determined. The least invasive mode of monitoring should be used. External monitoring should be adjusted to obtain an interpretable tracing; internal monitoring is generally not needed. Administration of an amnioinfusion requires an IUPC.

10) Change the mother's position?

Answer: Yes - When the mother is in a lateral position (right or left) or another non-supine position the uterus does not compress the vena cava or aorta; thus, maternal cardiac return and cardiac output are maximized, and blood flow to the uterus is optimal. Changing positions may also alleviate cord compression, if present.

11) Fluid bolus of at least 500 mL of lactated Ringers solution if not contraindicated?

Answer: No - First determine if the 500 mL fluid bolus at 1750 improved the fetal tracing. Lena has no contraindications; her intake and output should also be taken into consideration. There are data to suggest that increasing IV fluids will positively affect uterine blood flow and thus fetal oxygenation, even in women who are normotensive and well hydrated.

12) Administer supplemental oxygen at 10 L/min using non-rebreather mask and discontinue as soon as possible?

Answer: No - First allow time to determine if the least invasive interventions improved the fetal tracing. This intervention is used if other interventions such as position change and/or fluid bolus do not improve the tracing. Oxygen is discontinued as soon as possible based on fetal response.

13) Decrease or discontinue oxytocin?

Answer: Yes - Discontinue the oxytocin to reduce uterine activity. When oxygen is chosen for intrauterine resuscitation, it is assumed that other sources of potential fetal physiologic stress have been minimized; thus, oxytocin should not be infusing concurrently with maternal oxygen administration.

14) Administer tocolytic (e.g., terbutaline)?

Answer: No - Tocolytics may be administered as a temporary intrauterine resuscitation measure to reduce uterine activity.

15) Perform fetal scalp stimulation?

Answer: No - Fetal scalp stimulation would be helpful; however, Lena’s VEs should be kept to a minimum due to PROM. Fetal scalp stimulation is an indirect method used to evaluate oxygenation and acid-base status. An acceleration is associated with fetal-well-being; however, absence of acceleration does not predict fetal compromise. Fetal scalp stimulation is performed during segments of baseline, not during decelerations.

16) Correct maternal hypotension?

Answer: No - Hypotension is not present at this time. Hypotension decreases uteroplacental perfusion which becomes readily apparent in the fetal heart rate.

17) Administer a vasopressor (e.g., ephedrine)?

Answer: No - Ephedrine may be administered when a mother has hypotension secondary to regional analgesia or anesthesia. Administration of vasopressors is generally addressed in protocols and requires a physician order.

18) Administer an amnioinfusion?

Answer: Yes - Variable decelerations are present on Lena's fetal tracing. Amnioinfusion may be administered in an attempt to resolve variable decelerations. In the context of ruptured membranes, these decelerations may be caused by umbilical cord compression due to decreased amniotic fluid.

19) Notify primary care provider about findings and tracing?

Answer: Yes - The provider should be notified about the EFM tracing characteristics. In some situations, it is optimal for the clinician to remain at the bedside and request that someone else notify the primary care provider to request a bedside evaluation. The immediacy of the request is determined by the clinical situation. Some situations may require changes in the plan of care while others require bedside evaluation.

20) Notify the charge nurse?

Answer: Yes - Advise the charge nurse about Lena's fetal tracing and whether uterine resuscitative measures improved the fetal tracing. The charge nurse can coordinate or assist with communication with the perinatal team (pediatric/neonatal team, surgical team), if needed. She may also assist with or direct another staff member to assist with interventions, as needed.

21) Support maternal coping?

Answer: Yes - Calmly explain interventions as they are provided in an effort to enhance her understanding of the situation and to decrease potential anxiety.

Because the multiple position changes did not alleviate or improve the variable decelerations, the physician inserted an IUPC shortly after arriving at the bedside. The nurse initiated an amnioinfusion bolus at 1812.

By 1840, the variables began to decrease in frequency, no longer occurring with every contraction, variability began to improve to the moderate category. Lena requested and received an epidural at 2000, oxytocin was restarted, and her labor began to progress with subsequent vaginal delivery at 2315 of a baby girl with Apgars 7 at one minute and 9 at 5 minutes.
