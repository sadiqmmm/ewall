Case Study: Annabeth

Annabeth is a 35 year old woman, gravida 2, para 1, at 39 5/7 weeks gestation. Annabeth is married and works part-time as book-keeper at her family's restaurant. A previous pregnancy 5 years ago resulted in an operative vaginal birth at 38 6/7 weeks gestation after an elective induction and pushing for two hours.

During this pregnancy, a first trimester ultrasound revealed a pregnancy consistent with her last menstrual period; second trimester screenings were normal. Her pre-pregnancy BMI was 23. She gained 32 pounds during her pregnancy, within the recommended weight gain of 25–35 pounds. Annabeth's medical history is unremarkable; surgical history includes a laparoscopic cholecystectomy one month after her previous pregnancy 5 years ago.

Annabeth arrived on the labor and delivery unit accompanied by her husband at 0410. She stated that her contractions began around 0030, and her water broke at 0215. Her husband described the water as yellowish. Her contractions, much stronger after her water broke, are now every 3 minutes and last about one minute. Annabeth appears uncomfortable with her contractions and states her pain is 7 on a scale of 10.

Vital signs on assessment: temperature oral, 98.6° F (37° C); blood pressure, 124/70 mmHg; pulse 76 bpm; respirations, 20 breaths per minute. Vaginal examination reveals a cervix 4 cm dilated, 100% effaced, and fetal cephalic presentation at +1 station. At 0422, Annabeth is positioned on her left side with a wedge behind her back. The nurse palpated contractions as moderate and a soft resting tone. External electronic fetal monitoring was started. The nurse immediately noted a variable deceleration with a nadir of approximately 70 bpm, lasting 50 seconds from beginning to end, and then another variable deceleration with the next contraction. The nurse informed the charge nurse about the fetal tracing. The charge nurse came to assist so that the nurse could call the primary OB provider. The nurse called the provider about Annabelle's assessment findings and the fetal tracing that also revealed a fetal baseline of 135 bpm and minimal variability. The provider said he would arrive at the hospital in 20 minutes for a bedside evaluation, and requested that the nurse admit Annabeth for labor and obtain IV access due to the current FHR findings.

The nurse admitted Annabeth while the charge nurse initiated IV access and initiated a 500 cc fluid bolus. The fetal baseline remained 135 bpm; variability, minimal; and although the variable decelerations decreased to 40 seconds with a nadir to 80 bpm, the decelerations continued with each contraction. The provider arrived at 0455 and reviewed the tracing with the nurse and performed a VE which revealed the cervix 5cm dilated, 100% effaced and +1 station. The provider explained to Annabeth and her husband that he planned to order an amnioinfusion for decelerations and with their consent, he inserted an IUPC. Annabeth asked for an epidural, and the provider said he would call the anesthesiologist about the epidural. As the amnioinfusion began to infuse there were two more variable decelerations lasting 40 seconds with a nadir to 85 bpm when the anesthesiologist arrived to start the epidural.

Only respond to these questions in relation to the segments of Annabeth's tracing between 0545 and 0555:

1) What is the baseline fetal heart rate?

Answer: 135 - Approximate mean FHR rounded to increments of 5 bpm during a 10-minute window excluding accelerations and decelerations and periods of marked variability. There must be ≥ 2 minutes of identifiable baseline segments.

2) What is the variability?

Answer: Minimal - Amplitude range visually detectable but ≤5 bpm. (Greater than undetectable but ≤ 5 bmp).

3) Are accelerations present?

Answer: No.

4) What is the frequency of the contractions?

Answer: 2-2 ½ minutes - Contraction frequency is measured from the beginning of one contraction to the beginning of the next and is expressed in minutes.

5) What is the duration of the contractions?

Answer: 60-80 seconds - Duration is the length of the contraction and is described in seconds.

6) What is the category?

Answer: Category II, indeterminate - The variability is minimal and decelerations are present.

7) What type of periodic change occurred?

Answer: Variable decelerations.

What clinical interventions and communications are appropriate in the presence of these tracing characteristics and the assessment information about Annabeth?

9) Is internal fetal monitoring needed (FSE or IUPC)?

Answer: No - An IUPC is in place for the amnioinfusion, the fetal heart rate is recording and this is an interpretable tracing. An interpretable fetal tracing is important for the assessment of fetal status so that the need for uterine resuscitation can be determined. The least invasive mode of monitoring should be used. External monitoring should be adjusted to obtain an interpretable tracing; internal monitoring is generally not needed. Administration of an amnioinfusion requires an IUPC.

10) Change the mother's position?

Answer: Yes - Several position changes may be considered to alleviate potential cord compression. When the mother is in a lateral position (right or left) or another non-supine position the uterus does not compress the vena cava or aorta; thus, maternal cardiac return and cardiac output are maximized, and blood flow to the uterus is optimal. Changing positions may also alleviate cord compression, if present.

11) Fluid bolus of at least 500 mL of lactated Ringers solution if not contraindicated?

Answer: Yes - Annabeth has no known contraindications; her intake and output should also be taken into consideration. There are data to suggest that increasing IV fluids will positively affect uterine blood flow and thus fetal oxygenation, even in women who are normotensive and well hydrated.

12) Administer supplemental oxygen at 10 L/min using non-rebreather mask and discontinue as soon as possible?

Answer: No - The nurse should determine if the position change and amnioinfusion improves the tracing first, and then administer oxygen if the tracing has not improved. It is important to discontinue oxygen as soon as possible based on the fetal response.

13) Decrease or discontinue oxytocin?

Answer: No - Oxytocin is not being administered at this time.

14) Administer tocolytic (e.g., terbutaline)?

Answer: No - Tocolytics may be administered as a temporary intrauterine resuscitation measure to reduce uterine activity.

15) Perform fetal scalp stimulation?

Answer: No - In this instance with Annabeth, fetal scalp stimulation is not necessary because a VE has just been completed to insert an IUPC for the amnioinfusion. The clinician may choose to determine the impact of the amnioinfusion and then reconsider whether to perform fetal scalp stimulation. This intervention is an indirect method used to evaluate oxygenation and acid-base status. An acceleration is associated with fetal-well-being; however, absence of acceleration does not predict fetal compromise. Fetal scalp stimulation is performed during segments of baseline, not during decelerations.

16) Correct maternal hypotension?

Answer: No - Hypotension is not present at this time. Hypotension decreases uteroplacental perfusion which becomes readily apparent in the fetal heart rate.

17) Administer a vasopressor (e.g., ephedrine)?

Answer: No - Ephedrine may be administered when a mother has hypotension secondary to regional analgesia or anesthesia. Administration of vasopressors is generally addressed in protocols and requires a provider's order.

18) Administer an amnioinfusion?

Answer: Yes - Variable decelerations are present and an amnioinfusion has been initiated. Amnioinfusion may be administered in an attempt to resolve variable decelerations. These decelerations are often caused by umbilical cord compression due to decreased amniotic fluid.

19) Notify primary care provider about findings and tracing?

Answer: Yes - The provider should be notified about the EFM tracing characteristics. In some situations, it is optimal for the clinician to remain at the bedside and request that someone else notify the primary care provider to request a bedside evaluation. The immediacy of the request is determined by the clinical situation. Some situations may require changes in the plan of care while others require bedside evaluation.

20) Notify the charge nurse?

Answer: Yes - Advise the charge nurse about Annabeth's fetal tracing and whether uterine resuscitative measures and the amnioinfusion are improving the fetal tracing. The change nurse can coordinate or assist with coordination of the perinatal team, if needed. She may also be able to assist with or instruct another staff member to assist with interventions, as needed.

21) Support maternal coping?

Answer: Yes - Calmly explain interventions as they are provided in an effort to enhance her understanding of the situation and to decrease potential anxiety.

After the epidural, Annabeth described her pain as 2 on a scale of 10. The amnioinfusion continued using a volume pump, and the nurse weighed the underpads to assess return of infused fluid. At 0645 the provider returned to evaluate Annabeth. Annabeth stated she felt like pushing. The VE revealed that the cervix was completely dilated and the station was +2.

The nurse assisted Annabeth to push with every other or third contraction to maintain fetal reserve given the continued presence of variable decelerations with contractions. She delivered a baby girl at 0720 with a nuchal cord around the neck x 2, reduced at delivery of the head. The Apgars were 7 at minute and 8 at 5 minutes. The infant was placed skin-to-skin with Annabeth and began breastfeeding within the first hour of age.
